Making Mask

Kaduru and Ruk Attana woods are used to carve the masks. After the tree has been cut down, the tree should be exposed to the sun to dry thoroughly. After drying, carving begins using special tools designed for carving wood, such as taphole handsaws, planers, hand files, hammers and mallets. Never met mechanical equipment in the history of masks in Sri Lanka. All work is done by hand. This is the main reason why masks attract attention alongside other arts. After carving, the ancient creators used paint made from herbal plants to draw patterns onto the mask to pop facial reactions on the mask. It has been. Finally, a clear lacquer-like paint that is resistant to scratches and insects is applied to protect these masks.

Carving Tools

Kaduru wood is transformed into a mask by craftsmen who:

  • Dry the wood in the sun.
  • Chop and chisel the wood into the required shape.
  • Cure the wood for seven days over a smoky hearth to protect it from insect attacks.
  • Carefully smooth and craft the facial expressions of the characters in the wood.

Given below the step by step guide on Mask making process.

  • A good Kaduru tree (Strychnine tree, Strychnos nux-vomica, also known as poison nut. It has soft, easy to carve wood) is selected and felled.
  • The trunk dried to drain the sap and then when completely dry measured and cut into pieces.
  • The basic shapes of the masks are carved using a mallet, chisels and various other tools and in accordance to measurements in ancient scriptures
  • The wood is then smoked on racks in a large hearth to prevent future damage from insects.
  • The seasoned wood is carved into elaborate faces and expressions and smoothed.

Painting Mask

Traditional Sri Lankan masks are often brightly colored and depict gods, demons, animals, or other figures from mythology. You can research traditional designs online or come up with your own unique design. For that we have to use some tools like brushes of various sizes and sand paper. To paint a mask we should have a idea and design.


Here at follow there are major steps how to paint the mask.

  • If you are using a wooden mask, sand it lightly to smooth out any rough edges or imperfections.
  • Once you have your design planned out, start sketching it onto the mask using a pencil.
  • Use masking tape to block off areas you don't want to paint, if desired.
  • Begin painting the mask using your brushes and acrylic paint. You can mix colors on your palette or plate to create the shades you need.
  • Allow the paint to dry completely before removing the masking tape, if you used masking tape.
  • Once the paint is dry, you can add any additional details or accents using smaller brushes and finer lines.
  • When you are satisfied with your finished mask, allow it to dry completely before handling or displaying it.

Dance With Mask

The “Devil Dances” are an attempt to respond to the common belief that certain ailments are caused by unseen hands and that they should be chased away for the patient to get cured. If an individual or a family is not doing well, the village-folk believe that it’s because that person or the family is being harassed by unseen hands. A ‘thovil’ ceremony is the answer. The ‘thovil’ can be a simple ritualistic ceremony at home restricted to family and immediate neighbors or involving the whole village like the ‘gam-maduva’ or the ‘devol-maduva’ which is closely linked to the worship of gods. Masked dancers take part in at least two of the well-known ‘thovil’ ceremonies referred to as the ‘Maha Sohon Samayama’ and the ‘Gara Yakuma’. The mention of ‘Maha Sohona’ frightens the people since he is believed to be the demon of the graveyards. The performer disguises himself as a bear and wears a mask and a dress to resemble one. Often the ‘thovil’ involves the ‘sanni’ dances where all the dancers wear masks. The ‘daha ata sanniya’ refers to sixteen ailments with a demon being responsible for each one of them.



Dancers wearing masks take part in processions while at certain ceremonies, masks are used to depict different characters. Of later origin are the masks worn by children and teenagers at street performances during Vesak. Popularly known as ‘olu bakko’ for the simple reason that oversize masks are worn, these performances keep the younger-folk, in particular, entertained. The simple version of the devil dance ritual usually starts in the morning with the building of the stage, decorations and preparation of the costumes. The performers build an intricate stage before which the dancing commences. The stage consists of a wall made of freshly cut natural materials such as coconut palm tree and banana tree trunks. Depending on the region and the available materials the stage may also be coated with clay mud. The dances are accompanied by drummers which also herald the begin of the ritual. The distinctive sound ensures all neighbors turn up to take part. The full ritual usually lasts until the morning, with the dancers consuming betel-nut juice and drinking coke to stay awake. Dances can however also go on for multiple days.
